  • 09:10–11:00Ralph Hertwig — The ABCs of Bounded Rationality: Adaptive Rules for an Uncertain World
    More details
    TalkMPIB

    Abstract. Herbert Simon’s notion of bounded rationality represent, in my view, a research program with three aims: (1) to study the actual processes of decision making rather than as-if models of expected-utility maximization; (2) to analyze the heuristics and strategies used under genuine uncertainty and computational intractability, not merely under risk or ambiguity; and (3) to investigate how minds adapt to their environments by explicating the underlying mechanisms, rather than modeling the mind or the environment in isolation. In this talk, I will survey the repertoire of heuristics—the adaptive toolbox—available to individuals, institutions, collectives, and policymakers. I will then trace how the study of bounded rationality has developed in psychology and economics, and how it has often been recast primarily as a catalog of deviations from classical standards of rationality, with too little attention to how strategies fit their environments. Next, I will ask why people use heuristics, focusing on accuracy–effort trade-offs and, more importantly, on the surprising robustness of simple rules under limited knowledge. Finally, I will address the important objections that boundedly rational minds and heuristics will fail in strategic interactions.

    Bio. Ralph Hertwig is Director of the Center for Adaptive Rationality at the Max Planck Institute for Human Development in Berlin. Hertwig’s research focuses on models of bounded rationality such as simple heuristics, on the importance of learning and decisions from experience, on the measurement of risk preferences, and on ways to change people’s behavior for the better by boosting their competences. His articles have been published in top-tier scientific journals, such as Psychological Review, Behavioral and Brain Sciences, Psychological Bulletin, Trends in Cognitive Sciences, Psychological Science, Nature Human Behaviour, and PNAS. He has co-authored and edited five books, the newest one on Boost will come on the market in January 2027\. Hertwig has received various prizes for his work, the most important one is the Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz Prize, Germany’s most prestigious research award.     More details
    TalkGuest speaker

    Abstract. Trust your gut. Don’t rely on urges—analyze\! Decisions are either logical or psychological… Such popular maxims reveal a complicated, even confused approach to how one should make decisions. The sciences of decision theory and practice suggest that managers should leverage the power of mathematics, while also reserving a role for personal insights. But how exactly can that be achieved? Combining analysis and intuition sounds like trying to have one’s cake and eat it too; enticing but impossible. We believe that it is indeed possible to combine analysis and intuition to make decisions. And that doing so in a systematic way is now within reach. A slow yet powerful series of discoveries has culminated in a vision of decision-making that brings ever closer together mathematics, psychology and management in the form of analytical models of intuition. The concept that enables these advances is fast-and-frugal heuristics. The present piece (i) provides a background for fast-and-frugal heuristics; (ii) introduces their basics, outlining conditions under which fast-and-frugal heuristics perform well or not; (iii) challenges established beliefs and clears common misconceptions related to fast-and-frugal heuristics; (iv) outlines principles of smart (heuristics-based) management; and (v) surveys open questions to explore how the potential of fast-and-frugal heuristics for management mathematics can be fully realized.

    Abstract. The desire to assign a person to a class — friend or foe, trustworthy or not — is inherent in human nature. It provides bricks from which intelligence is built.   • 09:10–11:00Peter M. Todd — Foraging in Mind, with Heuristics and AI Collaborators
    More details
    TalkGuest speaker

    Abstract. How do we decide when to search for something better and when to stick with what we’ve got? People, like other organisms, must adaptively trade off between exploring and exploiting their environment to obtain the resources they need. This applies to whatever space they are searching, ranging from the external physical world, looking for objects or people or patches of food, to the internal information realm, looking for concepts in memory or knowledge online. Common underlying mechanisms may be used to address the explore/exploit tradeoff in each of these domains: People use similar heuristic strategies to decide when to keep looking and when to give up searching for patchy resources in space (e.g., for fish in a pond) and in mind (e.g., for words in a category). Moreover, the connections between search in these domains may have deep evolutionary roots, leading us to behave similarly in physical and digital environments. At the same time, the structure of the digital environment is changing rapidly through advances in AI, which can make heuristics that were previously ecologically rational become ineffective. But while AI can make some of the search tasks that humans have engaged in no longer necessary, there are ways that AI can enhance human search abilities rather than replace them, as I will point to in this talk.

  • 11:30–13:00Tomás Lejarraga — How experimental methods shaped views on human competence and rationality.
    More details
    TalkGuest speaker

    Abstract. Within just 7 years, behavioural decision research in psychology underwent a dramatic change: In 1967, Peterson and Beach (1967) reviewed more than 160 experiments concerned with people’s statistical intuitions. Invoking the metaphor of the mind as an intuitive statistician, they concluded that “probability theory and statistics can be used as the basis for psychological models that integrate and account for human performance in a wide range of inferential tasks” (p. 29). Yet in a 1974 Science article, Tversky and Kahneman rejected this conclusion, arguing that “people rely on a limited number of heuristic principles which reduce the complex tasks of assessing probabilities and predicting values to simple judgemental operations” (p. 1124). With that, they introduced the heuristics-and-biases research program, which has profoundly altered how psychology, and the behavioural sciences more generally, view the mind’s competences and rationality. How was this radical transformation possible? We examine a previously neglected driver: The heuristics-and-biases program established an experimental protocol in behavioural decision research that relied on described scenarios rather than learning and experience.

    Bio. Tomás is the director of the Decision Science Group at the Universitat de les Illes Balears in Spain and an associate researcher at the Center for Adaptive Rationality (ARC) in the Max Planck Institute for Human Development. He received his PhD from the Universitat Pompeu Fabra and was a postdoctoral fellow at the Carnegie Mellon University. He also spent four years as a research scientist at ARC in Berlin. He is interested in how individuals and groups explore, process, and integrate symbolic information and personally experience information when making decisions under risk and uncertainty.

  • 14:00–16:00WorkshopShahar Hechtlinger — How people make life’s most important decisions
    More details
    WorkshopMPIB

    Abstract. Judgement and decision-making research typically studies simplified, low-stakes choices among Western, educated, industrialized, rich, and democratic (WEIRD) samples, rather than real-life decisions that can transform lives—such as emigrating, getting divorced, or changing careers—made by people across the globe. This workshop will explore recent work that lays the conceptual and empirical foundation for understanding the psychology of these transformative life decisions, and give participants the opportunity to engage interactively with the topic. The first part will present a conceptual framework of transformative life decisions. Drawing on principles of bounded and ecological rationality, this framework identifies key dimensions of such decisions and specifies simple, largely non-compensatory choice strategies that address these dimensions. It will also cover the challenge that transformative life decisions pose to predicting the behavioural impact of new technological advances (e.g., AI companions). Building on this framework, the second part will present empirical work from five studies with over 4,000 participants spanning the UK, the US, India, Nigeria, and Germany. Using a mixed-methods approach combining manual text classification, large language models, and quantitative surveys, this work shows that transformative life decisions are common experiences across cultures and that people most commonly rely on simple strategies associated with subjective outcomes as favorable as complex deliberation. Yet in WEIRD countries, most individuals recommend complex, maximising strategies to others, revealing an “ideal–reality gap” that is absent elsewhere. These findings suggest that maximising is a culturally specific belief rather than universal behaviour, challenging a long tradition of applying laboratory-derived standards to real-world decision-making.

    Bio. Shahar Hechtlinger is a postdoctoral fellow at the Center for Adaptive Rationality (ARC) at the Max Planck Institute for Human Development. She studies search and choice processes in the real world, particularly under conditions of radical uncertainty and limited experience-based knowledge. Her current research explores how people make some of the most important decisions in their lives—such as emigrating or fleeing a country, ending a relationship, or engaging in acts of ‘civic heroism’ like disclosing sexual assault by a person in power. Her work is informed by a long-standing interest in philosophy of science and the meta-scientific questions of how the behavioural sciences study—and should study—consequential real-world phenomena. She recently received the prestigious de Finetti Award for her work on transformative life decisions.

  • 09:10–11:00Kamil Fulawka — Large language models accurately identify decision reasons in verbal reports
    More details
    TalkGuest speaker

    Abstract. Understanding the reasons behind human choices under risk is a central goal of decision scientists, but traditional methods relying on behavioural data are limited by strict invariance assumptions. We introduce a scalable analytical framework using large language models (LLMs) to analyze verbal reports and identify articulated reasons for choice between monetary lotteries. A validated LLM accurately identified predefined decision reasons in participants' free-text reports, aligning with their actual choices in 95% of trials. Our analysis reveals that the reasons behind people's decisions vary systematically and are driven more by the structure of the choice problem than by individual differences. Crucially, reasons identified from verbal reports yield more parsimonious and informative representations of decision processes compared to those inferred from choices alone; furthermore, problem-specific reason profiles achieve out-of-sample prediction accuracy that is competitive with established computational models. This work demonstrates that verbal reports are a rich data source and our analytical framework can unlock their potential, delivering results that challenge the field's foundational invariance assumptions and pave the way for more context-sensitive and interpretable models of human decision-making.

    Bio. I am a computational psychologist and researcher at the Center Synergy of Systems at TU Dresden. My work combines behavioural experiments and computational approaches, including large language models, to study decision-making. I focus on how emotions, heuristics, and digital environments shape behaviour in domains such as digital health and politics.

  • 11:30–13:00Levin Brinkmann — When Machines Expand Human Problem-Solving
    More details
    TalkMPIB

    Abstract. Many heuristics shaping our cognition are acquired culturally rather than discovered individually. Through social learning, useful strategies and behavioural traits are transmitted and preserved across generations. Today's AI systems are products of this same process. But the relationship is no longer one-directional. Machines are now feeding back into human culture in multifaceted ways. I will focus this talk on two prototypical effects.

    The first is adaptive adoption. We reproduce the "AlphaGo effect" in a lab-based experiment and show that machine-discovered strategies can persistently shift human problem-solving across generations. In particular, we show that these strategies propagate through human populations when they are non-trivial, learnable, and offer a recognizable advantage, and thereby expand the frontier of human problem-solving.

    The second is behavioural drift. I will present work on the "delve effect," showing that passive exposure to AI chatbots at scale measurably shifts human language, without requiring deliberate intent.

    Together, these findings shows that we are entering an age of machine culture — where AI is no longer merely a tool we use, but a force shaping our behaviour.

    Bio. Levin Brinkmann is a research scientist at the Center for Humans and Machines, Max Planck Institute for Human Development. He studies how humans and AI shape each other's behaviour and culture. His work spans cultural evolution in human-AI systems, emergent norms in LLM populations, and AI-driven discovery at the edges of human culture.

  • 11:30–13:00Dirk Wulff — Increasing Conceptual Clarity in Psychology with LLMs
    More details
    TalkMPIB

    Abstract. Psychology has a conceptual clarity problem: thousands of constructs and measures with unclear boundaries, overlapping definitions, and inconsistent terminology, often referred to as jingle-jangle fallacies. Sorting out these relationships empirically would require prohibitively costly research designs. In this talk, I show how large language models can help map the relationships between psychological constructs, measures, and observable behaviour at a scale that was previously impractical. I first present work showing that semantic embeddings from language models can predict empirical associations between survey items and scales, automatically detect jingle-jangle fallacies, and suggest more parsimonious taxonomies. I then present ongoing work that extends this approach beyond survey-based measures to behavioural tasks using task embeddings, vector representations that capture not only what a task asks but how people perform it. Together, these lines of work validate a generative approach that can help bring clarity and map out psychology's nomological net.

  • 14:00–16:00WorkshopRaluca Rilla — Detecting and Preventing LLM Pollution in Online Studies
    More details
    WorkshopMPIB

    Abstract. A substantial and growing share of online study responses reflects the influence of Large Language Models (LLMs). LLM-assisted responses pose a more serious threat because their convincing output can reshape response distributions in harmful ways. We term this phenomenon "LLM Pollution," composed of three parts: Partial LLM Mediation, Full LLM Delegation, and LLM Spillover, capturing the spectrum from participants using models to polish their answers, to agentic systems completing studies autonomously, to behavioural shifts among participants who suspect AI involvement. Because these forms vary in technical sophistication and human oversight, no single detection measure is sufficient. We will begin the workshop with a more detailed look into the LLM Pollution framework. Participants will then experience the capabilities of current agentic systems by deploying a simple browser-based agent to complete an online study (no prior experience with agents is needed). From there, participants will (i) develop in-survey measures for detecting LLM agents and LLM-assisted responses, and (ii) reflect on broader steps the research community must take to address this growing threat. We will conclude by synthesizing insights across groups and discussing the path forward.

  • 14:00–16:00WorkshopStefan Herzog & Julian Berger — Learning in human-AI interaction: Boosting vs. handicapping human competences
    More details
    WorkshopMPIB

    Abstract. Much attention in research and public discourse focuses on artificial intelligence and machine learning, while human learning remains an understudied aspect of human-AI interaction. In a recent meta-analysis (Berger et al., 2026), we found that only a minority of human-AI interaction studies allowed participants to learn from outcome feedback---meaning most participants could neither track their own or the AI's performance nor learn when and how to adaptively rely on AI. It is thus in our view unsurprising that, on average, humans with AI advice underperform relative to the AI alone. In contrast, our meta-analysis shows that humans tend to outperform AI when study designs allow for learning.

    In this workshop, we present our perspective on the unsatisfying state of the human-AI interaction literature and the paradigm shift needed to achieve the promise of human-AI synergy (i.e., humans with AI achieving superior outcomes compared to either acting alone). We argue for a research approach that fosters rather than handicaps human learning, and that can reveal the mechanisms that foster human-AI synergy. We present recent evidence for learning as a key driver of human-AI synergy (Berger et al., 2026\) and, through a series of short interactive exercises, demonstrate the boundary conditions such as task predictability, feedback clarity, and delay that shape the effectiveness of offering learning opportunities. The workshop aims to advance a perspective on human-AI interaction design that prioritizes boosting human competences (Herzog & Franklin, 2024).

    More details
    TalkMPIB

    Abstract. We know that delegating tasks to AI can drive impressive productivity gains. This is set to accelerate with increased usage of agentic AI systems, such as Open Claw. Yet, giving tasks to AI agents to complete autonomously also raises ethical risks. Are we inadvertently building systems that make it easier to delegate unethical behaviour?

    In this talk, I will share recent experimental work examining what happens when human principals use AI agents to perform common experimental tasks with clear incentives to cheat. Our findings reveal a troubling pattern: people are significantly more likely to request cheating when they don't have to explicitly ask for it, such as when they can induce cheating through high-level goal setting or supervised learning.

    Amid the emergence of large language models (LLMs), we also examined how instructing AI agents with natural language affected honesty, relative to human agents. While participants did not always ask AI agents to cheat more than human agents, there were stark results when it came to compliance with instructions. AI agents largely complied with fully unethical instructions while humans most commonly resisted. Given the apparent failure of inbuilt guardrails to inhibit cheating across popular LLMs, we explored bespoke guardrail strategies. With such strategies, compliance with unethical instructions could be curbed, but usually not eliminated. We will discuss design and policy strategies to mitigate ethical risks of delegating to AI.

    Abstract. Social media platforms act as de facto regulators of online speech, yet their governance decisions are increasingly shaped by political pressure rather than evidence—most visibly in the widening transatlantic divergence between EU regulatory tightening and US platform deregulation.     Abstract. The information environment shaping contemporary belief formation is not a neutral medium but an engineered system.   • 09:10–11:00Gerd Gigerenzer — On AI-Pollution: Will AI contaminate science?
    More details
    TalkMPIB

    Abstract. AI is celebrated as a disruptive technology. It is now beginning to disrupt science itself — and not for the better. I draw on my experience as Vice-President of the European Research Council (ERC) and on my research on paper mills. The ERC permits researchers to use large language models (LLMs) to write proposals, but forbids reviewers from using them to write evaluations. The second rule is increasingly ignored; the first has brought the ERC close to collapse. In 2025, submissions to ERC calls rose by an unprecedented 30–40% over 2024, largely driven by the use of LLMs. This mass production of proposals creates an intolerable burden on reviewers, and a predictable decline in the quality of peer review and funding decisions. Similar surges are being reported by other funding agencies and in AI conference submissions.

    The problem does not stop there. Paper mills now use LLMs to manufacture articles with fraudulent data at an industrial scale. Researchers under career pressure pay paper mills five-digit sums to be listed as authors on these fake papers in order to improve their metrics. Paper mills, in turn, bribe journal editors with six-digit sums to place these papers in special issues. Gold Open Access has unwittingly helped create this market, much as it has fueled predatory journals and enriched large publishing companies whose business model rewards volume over quality. And every academic who evaluates applicants by counting publications rather than judging their substance becomes, knowingly or not, an accomplice in this contamination of science by commerce. At a time when science is already under political and social attack, this contamination risks destroying something far more valuable than research budgets: public trust in science itself.
